### Lead (40-60 word direct answer for GEO)

When a Sub-Zero is running but not cooling, the cause is rarely the compressor. Nine times out of ten it's the evaporator fan, the defrost system, or a sealed-system restriction — and the diagnostic to tell them apart takes about ten minutes. Here's the same checklist I run on every "not cooling" call across South Florida.

---

### What you'll need

- Hand on the back grille
- Ear at the freezer wall
- Flashlight
- A timer (your phone)

That's it. No tools, no specialist gear. The first ten minutes of a Sub-Zero diagnostic is observational, not mechanical.

---

### Step 1 — Listen at the back grille (30 seconds)

Pull off the kick plate or step behind the unit. Listen.

**You hear a steady humming compressor?**
Compressor is running. The cooling system has power. The problem is downstream — air movement or sealed-system. Continue to step 2.

**You hear nothing?**
Compressor isn't running. Check the unit's display for an error code, check that the unit has power at the outlet, and check whether the unit is in defrost mode (it'll cycle back on after 20-30 minutes). If still nothing after 30 minutes, you have a compressor or relay issue — that's a service call.

**You hear clicking that doesn't lead to a steady hum?**
Compressor is trying to start and failing. That's a relay or starting capacitor issue. Service call.

---

### Step 2 — Feel the back grille (30 seconds)

Hand on the condenser area at the back of the unit (or behind the kick plate on Built-In models).

**Hot?**
Compressor is working hard. Air is being pushed across the condenser. Cooling system is engaged. Continue to step 3.

**Warm but not hot?**
Possible reduced airflow at the condenser — usually dust blockage. Pull the kick plate, check for dust accumulation. Vacuum the condenser if you can see dust. Then re-check in 30 minutes.

**Cool to the touch with compressor running?**
Reduced compressor output. Either the compressor is failing or the sealed system has lost refrigerant. Either way, service call.

---

### Step 3 — Listen at the freezer wall (60 seconds)

Open the freezer. Hold your ear close to the back wall (not touching). Stay still.

**You hear a soft whirring?**
That's the evaporator fan. Working. Continue to step 4.

**Silence (and the compressor is running)?**
This is the most common "not cooling" cause: evaporator fan motor failure. Cold is being made but not blown into the cabinet. Half my "not cooling" calls end here. Easy fix, common part. **Service call.**

**A scraping or rattling sound?**
Evaporator fan blade is hitting ice buildup. Usually means the defrost system has failed and ice has accumulated on the coil. Service call.

---

### Step 4 — Check the freezer for ice buildup (60 seconds)

Open the freezer wide. Look at the back wall and the floor.

**Ice on the back wall (more than just frost on packages)?**
Defrost system has failed. Defrost heater, defrost timer, or defrost thermostat. Common failure. Inexpensive parts. The ice buildup blocks airflow to the refrigerator section, which is why your fridge is warm. **Service call — but this is a fixable, normal failure.**

**No ice buildup?**
Continue to step 5. Defrost system is fine.

---

### Step 5 — Check the door seal (60 seconds)

Close the door on a dollar bill, halfway in. Try to pull the bill out.

**Bill slides out easily?**
Door gasket compression is failing. Warm air is leaking in continuously. The compressor is running constantly trying to compensate. Door gasket replacement is one of the highest-ROI Sub-Zero repairs we do. **Service call — but this is a small one.**

**Bill drags noticeably?**
Door seal is fine. Continue to step 6.

---

### Step 6 — Check the temperature setting (15 seconds)

I know — but you'd be surprised how many times I drive to a Coral Gables home and the thermostat got bumped during a kid's snack raid.

Refrigerator should be set 36-38°F. Freezer 0°F.

If the setting is off, correct it and wait 4 hours before declaring victory. If the setting is correct, continue to step 7.

---

### Step 7 — Check for an error code (30 seconds)

Modern Sub-Zero units self-diagnose. Look at the digital display.

| Code | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **E1** | Refrigerator section over-temperature |
| **E2** | Freezer section over-temperature |
| **E3** | Sealed-system pressure issue |
| **EE** | Electronic control board fault |
| **dr** | Door alarm (door open too long) |

If you see a code, write it down and call us. We'll pull the service bulletin for that specific code before the technician arrives.

## What "Not Cooling" Almost Never Is

After 15 years of Sub-Zero service calls, here's what "not cooling" is rarely:

- **Compressor failure** — true compressor failures are rare. Most "compressor failures" are actually fan motor or defrost board failures.
- **Refrigerant loss** — sealed system leaks happen but usually present as gradual temperature drift over weeks, not sudden failure.
- **Total electronic failure** — control boards fail in distinctive patterns (specific error codes, partial functionality), not "everything stops cooling."
- **Bad freon** — refrigerant doesn't go bad. It's a closed system. If refrigerant is the issue, it's because there's a leak.

When a customer tells me "I think the compressor is dying," I run this same diagnostic. It's the compressor maybe 5% of the time.
