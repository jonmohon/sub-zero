### Lead (40-60 word direct answer)

Salt-air corrosion is the #1 killer of marine Sub-Zero refrigerators. A residential Sub-Zero in a Coral Gables kitchen typically lasts 15-20 years; the same unit on a yacht in Miami Beach Marina lasts 6-8 if you skip maintenance. Here's the maintenance schedule that gets marine Sub-Zero units to 15+ years of service.

---

### Why Marine Sub-Zero Fails Faster

A Sub-Zero installed in a yacht looks identical to one in a kitchen. It isn't.

Three forces work against a marine unit constantly:

1. **Salt air ingress** corrodes electrical contacts, copper coils, and aluminum fan housings on a 5-10x faster timeline than residential
2. **Constant vibration** loosens compression fittings, refrigerant service ports, and electrical connections that would never move on a static install
3. **Power instability** — yacht inverters, shore-power transitions, and generator switching introduce voltage spikes and sags that Sub-Zero electronic control boards weren't designed to handle long-term

A residential maintenance schedule applied to a marine unit produces predictable failure at year 6-8. A marine-specific maintenance schedule routinely gets units to 15-20 years.

---

### The Marine Maintenance Schedule

Here's the schedule we recommend to every yacht owner with Sub-Zero refrigeration:

#### Every 60 days (you can do this)

**Visual condenser inspection.** Pull the kick plate. Look at the condenser coil. Even small visible salt deposits indicate active corrosion. If you see anything more than a thin dust film, it's time for a service visit.

**Door gasket wipe-down.** Marine humidity and salt air break down door gasket rubber. Wipe with a damp cloth (no chemicals) to remove salt residue. Visual check for cracking or deformation.

**Listen test.** Run for 30 minutes with the door closed. Listen at the back grille and at the freezer wall. New noises (clicking, scraping, squealing) are early warning signs.

#### Every 6 months (we do this)

**Condenser cleaning and contact-point inspection.** We pull the back panel, vacuum the condenser, brush the coils, and inspect every electrical contact for salt creep. Replace any contact set showing visible corrosion. This is the single highest-value maintenance procedure for marine units.

**Refrigerant leak check.** Pressure test the system to confirm no slow refrigerant loss from vibration-loosened connections. Tighten anything showing micro-leaks.

**Door gasket compression test.** Bill test plus visual gasket inspection. Replace if compression is failing.

**Fan motor bearing check.** Spin both evaporator and condenser fans by hand. Any roughness indicates bearing wear from salt-air exposure.

#### Annual

**Full electrical contact replacement on high-corrosion components.** Compressor terminals, water inlet valve contacts, control board input connections. We replace the contact sets proactively rather than waiting for failure.

**Door gasket replacement.** Marine door gaskets typically need replacement annually regardless of visual condition. Salt and humidity degrade the rubber compound even when it looks fine.

**Inverter and shore-power input check.** Verify voltage stability under both shore power and inverter modes. Install or verify surge protection if the unit is missing it.

**Sensor calibration.** Marine units drift faster than residential. Recalibrate temperature sensors with a calibrated thermocouple at three positions inside the cabinet.

#### Every 3-5 years

**Compressor pressure test.** Verify compressor output is at spec. Marine compressors can lose efficiency gradually from extended high-vibration duty.

**Sealed system service.** Refrigerant top-off and filter-drier replacement if the system shows signs of moisture infiltration (rare but happens on units with extensive prior service).

### What You Can Do Yourself

Most yacht owners can handle the 60-day visual inspections without specialist help. The key is **knowing what to look for** rather than just doing it as a checkbox.

When you pull the kick plate every 60 days, look for:
- **White or green crusty deposits on metal components** — that's salt or copper corrosion. Action item: schedule service.
- **Fuzzy growth on copper coils** — that's oxidation. Action item: schedule service.
- **Discoloration on door gasket rubber** — early sign of UV/humidity degradation. Action item: schedule replacement at next service.
- **Visible refrigerant oil residue near service ports** — slow leak. Action item: schedule service immediately.

Don't try to clean corrosion yourself. Marine-grade contact cleaner (CRC 2-26 or similar) is appropriate for some applications, but applied incorrectly it can short electronics or damage capacitors.

### When to Call for Service Between Maintenance Visits

Most marine Sub-Zero failures give early warning. Call us when you see:

- **Gradual cooling loss over weeks** — refrigerant leak, slow but accelerating
- **New clicking or scraping sound** — fan bearing or compressor relay degradation
- **Unit cycling more often than it used to** — door seal failure or condenser blockage
- **Frost/ice buildup not seen previously** — defrost system failure
- **Any new error code** — control board has detected something

Catching these early prevents the catastrophic failures (compressor seizure, control board destruction, total refrigerant loss) that turn a $400 service call into a $3,000 replacement.
