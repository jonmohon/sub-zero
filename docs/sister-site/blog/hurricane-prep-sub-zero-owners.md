### Lead (40-60 word direct answer)

Hurricane season in South Florida means power loss, voltage spikes, humidity surges, and storm-water exposure — all things Sub-Zero refrigerators handle differently than basic appliances. Here's the before-during-after playbook I give to every Miami-Dade and Broward customer whose Sub-Zero needs to weather a storm.

---

## Before the Storm (48-72 Hours Out)

### 1. Lower the temperature 5°F

Set your refrigerator from 38°F down to 33°F, and your freezer from 0°F down to -5°F. The colder starting point gives you more buffer when power fails. Do this 24-48 hours before expected outage so the unit has time to fully reach the new setpoint.

### 2. Fill empty freezer space with water bottles

Every empty cubic foot in your freezer is a cubic foot of warm air after power fails. Fill ziplock bags or water bottles 80% full and freeze them. They displace warm air, hold cold longer, and become drinking water if the storm runs long.

### 3. Move perishables to the freezer if you can spare them

Anything you'd lose to spoilage should go to the freezer if there's room. Frozen items hold safe temperature 2-3x longer than refrigerated items.

### 4. Photograph the contents

If you have homeowner's insurance with food-loss coverage, document everything before the storm. Your phone camera is enough.

### 5. Clean the condenser coils

This is the maintenance you've been putting off. A clean condenser runs more efficiently when power's restored — and a unit running efficiently bounces back to setpoint faster after an extended outage.

### 6. Check ice maker production

Top off the ice maker bin. Ice extends the cooling buffer once power is lost.

## During the Outage

### 1. Don't open the door

I know this is the obvious one. But here's the math: a Sub-Zero with the door closed holds safe refrigerator temperatures for 4-6 hours and safe freezer temperatures for 24-48 hours. Each door opening cuts that buffer significantly.

If you absolutely must open the refrigerator: have everything you need planned in advance, open and close in under 15 seconds, do not rummage.

### 2. Don't open it just to "check"

Owners' instinct during a long outage is to open the door and feel whether things are still cold. Don't. Trust the seal. The door alarm (if your unit has one) won't sound until temperatures actually exceed safe range.

### 3. If you have a generator

Sub-Zero refrigerators are not high-draw appliances at steady-state — running compressor draws are typically 4-7 amps for residential units. They run fine on a 5kW or larger portable generator.

**But:** the startup current draw is much higher (15-20 amps for a few seconds). If your generator is sized tight, the Sub-Zero may trip it on start. Use a generator that's at least 2x the steady-state draw.

**Critical:** never connect a Sub-Zero to a generator without a transfer switch or proper isolation. Backfeeding the grid through your panel is dangerous to utility workers and illegal.

### 4. After 4 hours without power

Refrigerator contents — judge each item by smell and feel before consuming. The "4-hour rule" from food-safety guidance is conservative for Sub-Zero, which holds cold longer than basic units, but it's a good baseline.

### 5. After 24 hours without power

Freezer contents — most items still safe if the unit hasn't been opened. Items with ice crystals still visible on them can typically be refrozen safely. Items that are completely thawed should be cooked immediately or discarded.

## After Power is Restored

### 1. Don't expect immediate cooling

The unit will run continuously for several hours to recover setpoint after a long outage. This is normal. Don't add new warm food until the unit has been at setpoint for at least 4 hours.

### 2. Check the display for error codes

Power restoration after long outages often surfaces error codes that weren't there before. Most common:

- **EE** — control board reset/fault from the power transition
- **E1 / E2** — over-temperature warning from the outage event itself (clears automatically once setpoint is reached)
- **F1 / F2** — fan motor fault (more concerning — call us)

### 3. Listen for new noises

Power surges during restoration can damage compressor relays, control boards, and fan motors. New clicking, buzzing, or grinding sounds in the first 48 hours after restoration are warning signs. Call us if you hear them.

### 4. Watch the cooling recovery

Refrigerator should reach 38°F within 4-6 hours of power restoration. Freezer should reach 0°F within 8-12 hours. If recovery is slower than this, something has been damaged by the outage.

### 5. Check the ice maker

Ice makers may need to be manually reset after extended outages. If no ice production resumes within 24 hours of power restoration, check the ice maker on/off switch and the water supply, then call if needed.

## What I See After Hurricane Season

Every November after hurricane season ends, our service calendar fills with the same call patterns:

### Pattern 1: Voltage-damaged control boards

Power restoration after extended outages often involves voltage spikes. Sub-Zero electronic control boards are sensitive — most failures show up 2-6 weeks post-storm rather than immediately.

**Symptom:** intermittent display issues, error codes that come and go, partial functionality loss.

**Prevention:** install a whole-home surge protector at your main panel. Cost: $200-400. Saves multiple Sub-Zero control boards over the unit's lifetime.

### Pattern 2: Compressor wear from extended-cycle recovery

Long outages mean the compressor runs continuously for hours during recovery. This isn't a problem in moderation. Multiple long outages per season add up.

**Symptom:** gradual cooling efficiency loss, longer cycle times, eventual compressor weakness.

**Prevention:** the only real prevention is reducing outage duration (generator) or accepting that hurricane seasons accelerate compressor wear by ~10-15% per major event.

### Pattern 3: Door gasket damage from humidity

Sustained high humidity during outages (no AC running) accelerates door gasket degradation. Salt-air homes near the coast see this most.

**Symptom:** loose or non-sealing door gasket, increased compressor cycling.

**Prevention:** post-season inspection. Replace if compromised — high-ROI repair.

### Pattern 4: Ice maker water inlet valve failures

Pressure surges in water lines during storm-related municipal work can damage water inlet valves.

**Symptom:** no ice production, leaking ice maker, or constant valve cycling.

**Prevention:** none easily. Just be aware that post-season ice maker problems often trace back to storm events.

- The unit doesn't reach setpoint within the recovery windows above
