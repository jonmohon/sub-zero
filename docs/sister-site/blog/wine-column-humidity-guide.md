### Lead (40-60 word direct answer)

Sub-Zero wine columns target 50-70% relative humidity to keep corks plump and seals tight. After 8-10 years, most units drift below 50% — invisible to the owner, slow death for the wine. Recalibration restores correct targeting in a single visit. Here's what's happening and how to fix it.

---

### Why Humidity Matters in a Wine Column

Wine bottles are sealed by cork. Cork stays elastic and forms a tight seal as long as it stays moist. Cork needs ambient humidity above ~50% to retain moisture.

In a wine column running below 50% RH:
1. Corks gradually dry out
2. Dried corks shrink slightly
3. Tiny gaps form between cork and bottle neck
4. Air enters the bottle
5. Wine oxidizes — flat, vinegary, eventually undrinkable

The kicker: this happens slowly enough that the owner usually doesn't notice until they open a bottle that should have been at peak and finds it past prime. By then, anything in the column with similar age has been similarly damaged.

A wine column running at correct humidity (50-70%) keeps corks elastic and bottles in the condition they were stored in.

---

### Why Wine Column Humidity Drifts

Sub-Zero wine columns control humidity through a combination of:
- **Evaporator coil temperature** (colder = lower humidity)
- **Defrost cycle frequency** (more defrost = more moisture release)
- **Door seal integrity** (poor seal = lower humidity)

After 8-10 years of operation, several things happen:
1. **Evaporator runs slightly colder** as the temperature sensor drifts and the control board compensates
2. **Door seals lose compression** from cycling and humidity flexing
3. **Defrost timing drifts** as the timer wears

The combined effect: humidity drops below the 50% target. Often into the 35-45% range. Wine ages faster than the owner intends.

---

### How to Tell If Your Humidity Has Drifted

Most Sub-Zero wine columns don't display humidity on the front panel. The unit may show temperature only.

Three ways to assess:

**1. Look at the corks on bottles you've stored long-term.** Pull a bottle that's been in the column for 2+ years. Look at the top of the cork through the foil capsule. If the cork shows pulling away from the bottle neck, drying, or visible gaps — humidity has been low.

**2. Buy a portable hygrometer.** Small digital hygrometers cost $15-25. Place inside the column for 24 hours. Read the average. Below 50% — humidity has drifted. Below 40% — significantly drifted.

**3. Schedule a service-level humidity check.** This is what we do as part of any wine column service. We use calibrated hygrometers at three shelf positions and assess targeting accuracy.

If your column is 8+ years old and you haven't had it recalibrated, assume it's drifted and schedule a check.

---

### What's Happening Inside the Column

Sub-Zero wine columns use a single evaporator coil to manage both temperature and humidity. The control logic targets a temperature setpoint (you choose 50-65°F typically) and the humidity follows from how the evaporator runs to maintain that temperature.

Two parameters control the coil behavior:
- **Defrost interval** — how often the coil thaws to release moisture
- **Coil run-time** — how long the coil runs per cooling cycle

If the temperature sensor reads slightly high (drift), the control board runs the coil more aggressively to compensate. More aggressive coil running means colder coil. Colder coil means more moisture frozen out of the cabinet air. Result: lower RH.

Recalibration corrects the sensor drift. The coil then runs less aggressively. Cabinet humidity rises back to target.

---

### How Recalibration Works

When we recalibrate a wine column, here's what happens on-site:

1. **Pre-service measurement.** We measure actual cabinet temperature and humidity at three shelf positions. Document baseline.
2. **Sensor verification.** We test the temperature sensor against a calibrated thermocouple. If it reads more than 1°F off, we either replace it or compensate at the control board.
3. **Door seal check.** Bill test plus visual inspection. Replace if compromised.
4. **Defrost timing assessment.** If defrost is over- or under-cycling, adjust the control board parameters.
5. **Re-measurement after stabilization.** Bring the unit to setpoint. Wait 30-45 minutes. Re-measure temperature and humidity.
6. **Final adjustment.** Tune until humidity is in the 55-65% target range across all three shelf positions.
7. **Documentation.** Final temperatures and humidities recorded on your invoice.

Total time on-site: typically 60-90 minutes. Sometimes less if the unit only needs sensor adjustment, more if door gasket replacement is also needed.

---

### How Often Should You Recalibrate

For wine columns under 5 years old: probably no recalibration needed unless you observe symptoms.

For wine columns 5-10 years old: recalibration check every 2 years is reasonable preventive maintenance.

For wine columns over 10 years old: annual recalibration is wise, especially if the column holds a meaningful collection.

For wine columns over 15 years old: annual at minimum, plus consider sensor replacement (preventive) at the same visit.

---

### Other Wine Column Issues That Affect Humidity

A drifted humidity reading sometimes points to a different underlying issue. We check for these on every wine column service:

**Failing door seal.** Compromised gasket lets warm dry air in continuously. Replacing the gasket immediately corrects humidity.

**Compressor short-cycling.** Compressor that runs too briefly per cycle never reaches steady-state cooling. Coil never frosts/defrosts properly. Humidity gets erratic.

**Refrigerant low.** Slow leak reduces cooling capacity. Coil runs longer to compensate. More moisture pulled from the air. Sealed-system service required.

**Frosted-over evaporator.** Defrost system failure causes ice buildup on the coil. Air can't pass over it. Humidity goes erratic. Defrost system service.

These all present as "humidity is wrong" but require different fixes. On-site diagnostic determines which.
